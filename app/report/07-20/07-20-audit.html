The short version

The app is further along than it looks from the outside. Most of what was asked for in the meeting is genuinely built and working — the Open Play and Events tabs, real live listings, walk-in bookings for venue owners, venue claiming, coach and organizer sign-up, and the whole social section. None of that needs to be re-done.

The problems that remain are mostly not missing screens. They are missing rules — what the system does when something goes wrong, or when two people do the same thing at once. This matches exactly what the meeting predicted: a complete-looking interface is not the same thing as a launch-ready product.

Two of the biggest remaining items are waiting on business decisions from your side, not on development work. Those are the ones most likely to delay the launch.

The seven things that would hurt us on launch day

01

Anyone can join an organizer's Open Play session — no approval needed

The meeting was clear that joining should put a player on a waiting list, with the host deciding who gets in. That works for sessions created by ordinary players. For sessions created by organizers and venue owners, it doesn't work at all — anyone who taps "join" is instantly confirmed, there is no limit on numbers, and everyone who taps lands straight in the private lobby and group chat.

Why this matters most: organizer and venue sessions are the ones PickleBallers actually earns money from. The paid path is the unprotected one.

Decided 20 July: build it properly — waiting list, host approves or rejects, a real cap on numbers, and the lobby locked to approved players only.

02

Player-created sessions confirm people automatically

The approval feature exists here, but it is switched off unless the host remembers to turn it on. So by default, joining still auto-confirms — the exact thing the meeting said must not happen.

Decided 20 July: switch it on by default.

03

Two people can book the same court at the same time

If two players book the last available slot within a moment of each other, both bookings can go through. The system checks whether the slot is free, then saves the booking — and another person can slip in between those two steps.

This is already known internally and it is a small, cheap fix. It is also the single most damaging item on this list. Nothing erodes trust in a booking platform faster than two people turning up for the same court.

04

Bookings can get stuck forever, blocking a court

When a booking needs the venue owner's approval and that owner simply never responds, the booking never expires. It sits there indefinitely and the court stays blocked for everyone else.

Related: the clean-up that should clear stale bookings only runs when a player happens to open their own bookings screen. Nothing runs on a schedule, so a court can be silently unavailable for days.

05

Players are never told when a booking is rejected

If a venue owner turns down a booking request, the player receives nothing — no notification, no email. They find out by checking the app, or they don't find out and show up anyway.

The system also has no proper "rejected" status. A rejection is recorded the same way as a cancellation, so the two can't be told apart in reports.

06

Payments only work in test mode

Payments currently run on a demo card number that always succeeds — normal for development. The problem is what happens when it is switched to real mode:

  • Bookings would take the payment as "pending" and never confirm. The booking just hangs.
  • Coach and organizer subscriptions are worse — the system grants the paid role even though no payment was taken. People would get paid features free.

Blocked on the payment decision. Nothing here can be finished until we know whether GCash is manual, semi-automated, or a full gateway.

07

Venue staff can change prices

There is a proper staff role, and it correctly blocks staff from revenue reports and financial data. That part works.

But the price controls are only hidden in the interface — they aren't actually blocked underneath. A staff member with some technical knowledge could change slot pricing and venue rates. Where the front desk is a part-time or casual hire, that is a genuine risk.

Five more things needed before launch

Senior and PWD discounts don't exist

There is nothing in the system for senior citizen or PWD rates — not a setting, not a field, not a checkbox. Today the only way to give the discount is for staff to type a different amount by hand, which means it is never recorded as a discount and never appears in any report.

In the Philippines the 20% senior and PWD discount is required by law, so this is a compliance issue, not a convenience feature.

Decided 20 July: build the complete system — venues configure their own category rates, and both staff bookings and player self-service bookings can apply them.

Venue owners can accidentally create duplicate venues

The claiming process works well — an owner can find an existing venue, claim it, and an admin reviews the claim.

What's missing is the prompt. When an owner types a venue name that already exists, the system doesn't notice or say anything. So owners will keep creating duplicates of venues already listed, and you will be cleaning them up by hand.

Player filters reset constantly

The filters themselves are excellent and cover everything requested — dates, distance, gender, skill, cost. But nothing is remembered. Worse, switching between the Open Play and Events tabs actively wipes them back to default, so players re-configure every single time.

Good news: this is a small fix. The hard part — the filters — is already built.

The map still clumps together on mobile

The specific complaint from the meeting is unresolved. Venue pins are still drawn one by one, so venues near each other overlap into a blob.

Some improvements did land — the map zooms sensibly and you can slide the list up over it — but the overlapping-pins problem itself hasn't been addressed. A full-screen map does exist in the app, but no button anywhere leads to it.

Coaches who pay stay invisible

A coach can sign up and pay. But their profile is created as "not yet verified" and "not yet listed" — and there is no way for anyone, including an admin, to change that. The button doesn't exist.

So a coach pays for a subscription and is never visible to players. The sign-up page even advertises a "Verified Coach badge" that nobody can actually receive.

Worth fixing, but not launch-critical

  • The brand colours are copied in eight different places. This matters for timing — when Marvin's new design arrives, applying it means making the same change eight times instead of once, and the worst offenders are the venue owner screens, which is exactly where the new design needs to reach. Tidying this up before the redesign arrives will save real time.
  • Too many routes to the same place. Four different map and venue screens, two different booking screens, and the Home "Book Court" button leads to the identical screen as the Nearby tab. This is the duplicate-navigation confusion raised in the meeting — still there.
  • Missing endings to the booking story. No-shows aren't handled at all. Refunds show a "processing, 5–10 business days" message but no refund actually happens. And a player can move a weekday booking to a busy weekend slot without paying the price difference.
  • Most notifications aren't connected. The notification system itself works well, but only two events trigger one. Players aren't told when a booking is confirmed, cancelled, paid, refunded, or about to expire.
  • Every walk-in booking leaves a stray ₱0 price tag on that time slot, which could make the slot free for whoever books it next.
  • Finished features nobody can reach. The organizer Tournaments section and the Venue Requests screen are fully built, then hidden behind a disabled menu. Either turn them on or remove them — right now they are invisible work.

Deliberately left undone

Held — not worked around

The database has 181 real venues and zero real courts.

The import tool works and fully supports courts. The courts spreadsheet we were given is empty — column headings, no rows.

We could have papered over this by auto-generating court entries from the venue records. You decided on 20 July not to do that. We are still in development, so it stays visibly undone rather than hidden behind placeholder data.

It does mean booking can't be properly tested against real data until the real spreadsheet arrives — which is on the client action list from the meeting.

Still open from earlier reports

  • The official BIR receipt screen for venue owners — the back end is finished, the screen was never built. The tracking sheet incorrectly marks this as done.
  • Collecting Open Play join fees and tracking organizer payouts — waiting on the payment decision.
  • Player-created game listings show a price the joining player doesn't actually pay. It reads as though joining costs ₱350. This is a live hazard in any demo.
  • The "Events" tab shows player-created games, not organizer tournaments — not what the name suggests. Needs a decision: move tournaments in, or rename the tab.
  • 17 venue-owner features are still marked "built but not tested." No later report ever confirmed them. If anyone asks whether everything is working, this is the honest answer — untested, not unbuilt.

What we need from you

Two of the seven launch-day problems cannot be finished without business decisions. These are on the critical path — more so than any of the remaining development work.

  1. The payment decision. Who collects the money, when PickleBallers takes its cut, and whether GCash is handled manually or through a proper gateway. Payments, refunds, join fees and organizer payouts are all waiting on this.
  2. The court spreadsheet. Until it arrives we have venues without courts, and the booking flow can't be tested against real data.

Everything else on this list is ours to build.

Honest assessment of the end-of-July target

It's achievable, but only if the scope freezes now.

The remaining work is mostly small and well-understood. The double-booking fix, the stuck-booking fix and the missing rejection notice are each modest pieces of work with large consequences. The Open Play approval flow and the senior/PWD pricing system are the two substantial builds, and both are now decided and ready to start.

The real risk isn't the code. It's the two decisions above, plus the 17 owner features that have never been tested by a human. Testing time needs to be in the plan, not squeezed out of the end of it.
